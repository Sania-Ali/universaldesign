Fashion Icon:
Jillian Mercado

Jillian Mercado is a prominent Latinx model who advocates for changing representation norms in the fashion industry. She is an IMG Model and has been featured by Diesel, Nordstorm, Target, and other brands. Mercado was also the first disabled cover star for Teen Vogue's 2018 September issue. In the picture next to the text, Mercado shines in a sparkling gold outfit and headdress during the Blonds Fall 2020 fashion show. To learn more about her runway moment, click "Jilian Mercado"

Tommy Adaptive

Runway of Dreams and Tommy Hilfiger are partnering to create an adaptive clothing collection for children. Examples of adaptive clothing include replacing buttons, zippers, and other fastenings with washable magnet-like fixings. Waistbands and sleave and pant lengths are also fully adjustable. Read more about and shop the collection by clicking, "Tommy Hilfiger"

Fashion Icon: Sinéad Burke

Sinéad Burke is fashion icon. She was the first little person to attend the Met Gala in 2019, and sported a custom-made Gucci dress. Burke is also the first little person to be featured on the cover of Vogue Magazine (September 2019 cover). As a prominent disability rights activist, Burke believes, "We need to constantly be asking what voices are not in the world, which perspectives are not being considered, and make sure that change occurs with as much intersectionality as possible." Read more about Burke, by clicking, "Sinéad Burke."

Fashion Icon: Madeline Stuart

Madeline Stuart is the world's first professional model with Down syndrome. She has been featured in numerous publications and has modeled in dozens of fashion shows around the world. In 2017, she launched her own fashion label: 21 Reasons Why. To learn more about Stuart click, "Madeline Stuart," and to shop her collection click, "21 Reasons Why"

"Seated Collection"

Fashion student Lucy Jones created a collection designed for self-propelled, seated individuals. She was awareded Parson School of Design's "Womenswear Designer of the Year" in 2015. Jones described pant design as the most complicated aspect of the collection. Challenges she faced include considering how kneecaps change shape when bent, removing excess fabric, reinforcing elbows, and adjusting the length and space of the garments. Click, "Lucy Jones" to learn more about the collection.  

Real Life Mannequins

The JCPenny store in New York City featured a unique manequin display inspired by real people: Dawna Callahan, who uses a wheelchair due to incomplete paralysis; Neil Duncan, a former Army paratrooper who lost parts of both of his legs in an explosion in Afghanistan; Ricardo Gil, who has dwarfism; Desiree Hunter, a 6-foot-1½-inch college basketball player; and Beth Ridgeway, who is plus-size. Read more about the story by clicking, "JC Penny store" in the text.

Fashion Icon: Aimee Mullins

Aimee Mullins is a fashion model and double amputee. Her career in fashion began when she opened for Alexander McQueen's Spring 1999 show. Mullins has also shot with top photographers, including Steven Klein and Inez and Vinoodh. According to Mullins, "When I do wear prosthetics that really play with the aesthetic function, it changes the conversation. It changes how I feel about my body, it changes how people interact with me... Fashion is the perfect arena to explore these larger societal conversations around inclusivity." To read more about her career, click "Aimee Mullins".

Victorian Wooden Legs

These intricately carved Victorian knee-length boots with a Louis heel are custom-made prosthetics designed by Alexander McQueen, Bob Watts, and Paul Ferguson. Model Aimee Mullins wore these solid one-block wooden legs during the Spring/Summer 1999 McQueen fashion show. Victorian literature was the inspiration for the design.
