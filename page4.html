3D Printing in a Pandemic

The 3D printing community has mobilized during the COVID-19 pandemic. Companies such as BCN3D, Airwolf3D, and Volkswagen are printing custom medical components. Other companies and individuals are printing hands-free door handle attachments, face shield frames, face masks, swabs, and other neccessary equipment. To keep up with all the news, click "3D printing community" in the text.

Clear Face Masks

Student Ashley Lawrence designed a face mask with a clear plastic window where the person's mouth is. Lawrence created the masks after realizing that there was a limited supply of face masks that are suitable for people who are deaf or rely on lip-reading or ASL as a way of communication. To read more about her story, click "Ashely Lawrence" in the text.

Tele-medicine Robot

Children's National Health system announced a new Wi-Fi-enabled robot as part of its tele-medicine program in 2019. The robot, named Dr. Bear Bot, is 7-months old and stands 5 feet and 6 inches. The robot works as a virtual liason between patients, attending, nurses, and physicians as part of the tele-cardiac ICU unit. To learn more, click "Dr. Bear Bot" in the text.

Maker Therapy

Gokul Krishnan is pioneering the design of patient-centric innovation spaces in pediatric settings. He has started a movement called, Maker Therapy, that brings mobile makerspace tools, such as 3D printers and circuits, to hospital settings. Patients and families have designed inventions including nightlights, mood necklaces, and doorbells. To learn more about the movement, click "innovation spaces" in the text.

Textile Monitors Vital Signs

MIT researchers have developed a washable sensor-embedded fabric that can monitor physiological conditions such as temperature, heart-rate, respiration, and physical movement. The garment can transmit this data wirelessly to a smart-device. Researchers are testing prototype shirts and envision use in personalized telemedicine. Click, "sensor-embedded fabric" to learn more.

Low-tech tools for Parkinson's

Mileha Soneji is a product designer who invented a No-Spill Cup and a staircase illusion for her uncle who is diagnosed with Parkinson's. The No-Spill Cup is designed with an inward curve to prevent spilling during uncontrolled hand tremors. The staircase illusion is made from black and white sheets of A3 paper stuck together with tape. The illusion takes advantage of the fact that for many Parkinson's patients, climbing stairs is easier than walking on flat surfaces. Click "Mileha Soneji" to learn more about the products.

Patient Innovation

Patient Innovation is an online platform for patients and caregivers to connect and share solutions to health-related problems. Solutions are sorted by condition, symptom, location, activities, devices, and therapy. Users of the site are part of a global creative community.
